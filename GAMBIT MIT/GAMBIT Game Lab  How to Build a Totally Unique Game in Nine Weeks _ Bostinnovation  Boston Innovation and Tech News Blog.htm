GAMBIT Game Lab: How to Build a Totally Unique Game in Nine Weeks

Kyle Psaty | March 31st, 2010

Editor’s Note: This post was updated for clarity on the evening of March 31, 2010, a few hours after it was posted.

GAMBIT: Where awesome minds make awesome games

Step 1) Get into MIT. Just kidding. The summer program at MIT GAMBIT Game Lab also accepts students from other colleges. GAMBIT is a collaborative effort between MIT and the government of Singapore to “explore new directions for the development of games as a medium.” GAMBIT was recently named one of the Top 10 video game design programs in the country by the Princeton Review.

Each summer, students from Singapore and MIT get together at GAMBIT to create totally unique video games during a nine-week period. I caught up with a few students involved with GAMBIT at last weekend’s PAX East event at the Hynes Convention Center.

“Innovation. That’s what we’re all about,” said Stephie Wu, a computer science student at MIT I spoke with. “Every game is linked to a research topic. We just want to do things in new ways.”

Another student I met was Anna Loparev, a senior at Wellesley College and the designer behind a pair of twin games developed at the lab last summer called Waker and Woosh.

“Each team has a research goal. The goals are based on proposals that the game lab receives earlier on. They decide which ones they want to do for the summer and give those goals to us,” said Loparev. “Generally, a team consists of a designer (Loparev), a producer, quality assurance, programmers, artists and some teams have an audio artist. We also had a graduate student and an embedded staff member on our team.”

Both Waker and Woosh each consist of 12 levels of platform-style gaming, but this isn’t Donkey Kong.

Sure, users play by navigating their avatars from the ground to the top of each level, much like in the original Donkey Kong, but rather than focusing on the timing of each obstacle, users have to “figure out” how to create pathways from one platform to the next without any instructions.

Screen Shot 1: Waker

“The concept was proposed by Scot Osterweil [an instructor in MIT's Comparative Media Studies department]. He does a lot of educational games that promote exploration so kids can learn things on their own. Our goal was to create a game that would teach middle school and high school students about displacement and velocity in a non-direct way,” explained Loparev. “That way, they would get some intuition about it, even though they might not know exactly what displacement-velocity is. When they go into the classroom it could seem less intimidating because they’ve seen it before.”

In case you don’t know what displacement and velocity are, they are physics and mathematics topics closely tied to calculus. Essentially, velocity is the derivative of displacement, but you don’t need to understand any of that to have fun with Waker and Woosh, both of which are PC-based.

Game play consists of left and right movement by way of the computer’s directional pad, but users must also grab items and jump in order to move from one platform to the next. Unlike other platform games, each time the avatar grabs a special orb on in the level, the game begins to draw a graph between two platforms. (See Screen Shot 1)

Screen Shot 2: Waker

The user must carry the orb left and right to control the rise and fall of the graph, and in later levels, they must stand, walk and run in order to direct the graph. Once the graph is drawn, the orb is dropped and the user can now walk on the graph that was drawn to advance to the next platform and complete the level. (See Screen Shot 2)

It sounds complex and boring, but I suppose that’s why GAMBIT made it so instructions aren’t necessary.

In actuality, it’s pretty addictive and Waker was recently named a finalist in the annual Indie Game Challenge. Although it ultimately didn’t win, the nomination is a testament to the uniqueness of the game.

So how’d they make it?

“We had milestones every week or so that we had to meet,” said Loparev. “We used the scrum method to complete the process. Every morning, we would meet to talk about what we were working on that day and what needed more work.

“One of the great things about the game’s development was that we built the core mechanic at the beginning. Picking up the orb and drawing the graph based on that was something that came pretty early. We new we wanted to draw a graph based on displacement and velocity, so we were able to move pretty quickly.”

What’s next for Waker and Woosh?

Both games are nearly identical, except Waker has a narrative and Woosh does not. MIT hopes to deploy both games in a study sometime in the future to determine if a storyline helps kids learn while playing games.

MIT GAMBIT Game Lab is currently preparing for this summer’s development sessions, and all of the games they’ve already developed are available to play (or download) for free at the GAMBIT website. I’d definitely suggest trying out Waker, but another game created by GAMBIT that’s earned some recent acclaim is CarneyVale: Showtime, which is now available for Xbox LIVE Community Games. You can pick that game up on your Xbox 360 for 240 MS Points (or $3) from now through the end of April, when the price will go up. It’s being offered at a discounted rate in celebration of PAX East.
